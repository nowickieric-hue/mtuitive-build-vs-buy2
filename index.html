02 · THE EVIDENCE

What the peer-reviewed literature says about synoptic reporting.

The evidentiary foundation for structured surgical oncology reporting is consistent and compounding. Synoptic reports are more complete, more reliable, faster to abstract, and better suited for downstream registry and quality use.

Source Key finding
Stogryn et al. — Scoping review (2023) Am J Surg · 32 included studies · n=4,353 screened View on PubMed ↗ Synoptic reports achieved 80 ± 19% overall completeness vs. 47 ± 18% for narrative reports (p < 0.001). Critical cancer elements — tumor location (89% vs. 51%), presence of metastases (96% vs. 36%), and resection margins (87% vs. 39%) — all significantly higher in synoptic format.
Robertson & Vergis — Rectal cancer study (2020) Am Surg · n=80 reports, prospective + matched cohort View on PubMed ↗ Synoptic reports scored 76 ± 4 vs. 41 ± 19 on validated quality indicator checklists (p < 0.01). Data extraction time was nearly halved: 3:46 vs. 6:21 minutes per case.
Maniar et al. — Colon cancer study Ann Surg Oncol · n=160 reports View on PubMed ↗ Synoptic reports scored significantly higher on quality indicators (18.6 ± 1.3 vs. 9.2 ± 3.6, p < 0.01). Data extraction time: 2:32 vs. 4:01 minutes. Interrater agreement was also significantly higher.
Zaveri et al. — ACS surgeon survey (2024) Ann Surg Oncol · n=377 surgeons (2021), 380 (2023) View on PubMed ↗ Average scores on surgical technique questions exceeded 80%, but scores on CoC accreditation standard-specific questions dropped as low as 53% for colorectal and 54% for melanoma. The gap is in standards knowledge — not surgical skill.
Hieken et al. — Annals of Surgical Oncology (2022) CSSP technical standards reference View on PubMed ↗ Synoptic operative reports improve semantic clarity, provide uniformity of data capture, and are well-suited for incorporation into quality measures for large registries such as the National Cancer Database.
Saraswathula et al. — JAMA (2023) JAMA · 162 metrics · 108,478 person-hours analyzed View on PubMed ↗ Quality metric reporting is expensive and time-consuming. Given the resources required, the implementation method matters as much as the metric itself: claims-based and chart-abstracted methods are dramatically more costly than electronic capture.
Bottom line. The literature consistently shows synoptic reports are more complete, more consistent, faster to abstract, and better suited for downstream quality and registry use. The implementation method — and how it is maintained over time — determines whether your program captures those benefits.

06 · THE ACCREDITATION MANDATE

What's currently in force, and what's coming next.

The decision to build or buy is, ultimately, an accreditation risk-management decision. These are the requirements currently in force or imminent — each links directly to its source.

CoC
Commission on Cancer · Standards 5.3–5.8
  • Synoptic reporting required for breast, colorectal, lung, and melanoma surgery (effective Jan 1, 2023)
  • 80% compliance threshold per standard, in force for 2025+ site visits
  • PDF fallback option eliminated as of Jan 1, 2026
  • 2026: expansion to additional disease sites
  • Surgeon awareness of standards as low as 53–54% in ACS national survey
CoC Operative Standards
NAPBC
National Accreditation Program for Breast Centers
  • Standard 2.17 requires documentation of specific intraoperative and pathologic data elements for breast cancer surgery
  • Programs must demonstrate systematic data capture and quality review — not individual surgeon compliance
  • Optimal Resources for Breast Care (2024 Standards) effective Jan 1, 2024
NAPBC Standards
NAPRC
National Accreditation Program for Rectal Cancer
  • Structured documentation required for TME quality, resection margins, lymph node harvest, and operative technique
  • Aligns with CoC Standard 5.4 (rectal resection)
  • Optimal Resources for Rectal Cancer Care (2026 Standards) effective Jan 1, 2026
  • Compliance thresholds raised to 90% for several standards in 2025
NAPRC Standards
STS
Society of Thoracic Surgeons · GTSD
  • Registry participation requires structured data submission for cardiothoracic procedures
  • GTSD fields align with CoC Standard 5.5 (lung resection) synoptic elements
  • mTuitive procedure mapping reduces registry abstraction burden vs. manual abstraction
  • Nearly 800,000 general thoracic procedure records in the GTSD
STS National Database

The accreditation landscape is not static. Each program has tightened requirements in recent years, and all are expected to continue. A build-your-own solution locks a cancer program into an internal maintenance cycle that tracks the ACS release calendar — not the EHR vendor's.

07 · RECOMMENDATION & FRAMEWORK

The case rests on four pillars.

Based on the clinical evidence, the accreditation requirements, the stakeholder impact, and the financial model — the case for mTuitive Surgery over an EHR-native build comes down to four things.

Speed and certainty of compliance

mTuitive can be live in weeks, with zero EHR development hours, achieving 95–99% compliance from day one. EHR-native builds take 12–24 months and deliver no mandatory compliance logic at the point of care.

Total cost of ownership

EHR build costs are routinely underestimated. $78,520–$157,040 initial plus $49,830–$63,420 in annual maintenance exceeds mTuitive subscription cost within 1–2 years — before remediation events ($53,605 each) or disease site expansion ($45,300–$75,500/site).

Data quality and downstream utility

Synoptic reports achieve 80% completeness vs. 47% for narrative. mTuitive's structured, concept-mapped data is immediately mineable for quality dashboards, registry submission, and real-world evidence — without additional EDW development.

Accreditation risk management

Cancer center leadership cannot afford to manage CoC, NAPBC, NAPRC, and STS compliance reactively. One below-threshold event carries $53,605 in direct labor cost, board-level escalation, and reputational risk with ACS surveyors. mTuitive's mandatory logic systematically eliminates this exposure.

When an EHR-native build can make sense

There are programs for which a build path is reasonable. The honest answer:

  • Your program has fewer than 3 disease sites with fewer than 200 annual cancer surgery cases, and your IT team has bandwidth and CoC-specific clinical expertise.
  • Your organization has already built validated Epic SDEs or Cerner discrete data fields for all required synoptic elements, with a dedicated informatics resource for annual updates.
  • Your EHR implementation is complete and stable, with no competing priorities for at least 18 months.

For all other programs — particularly those mid-EHR-migration, those with multiple disease sites, or those at near-term audit risk — mTuitive Surgery represents the lower-risk, lower-cost, and faster path to sustained compliance.
